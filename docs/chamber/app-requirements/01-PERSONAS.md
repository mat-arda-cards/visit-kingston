### Rosa Delgado

> _"I don't need another app to babysit — I need the whole internet to stop telling people we're open when we're closed, and to send the ferry crowd up my hill hungry."_

I'm Rosa. My husband and I have run Los Compadres Cantina for 18 years, up the hill off the main strip in Kingston. It's a family place — my son cooks, my sister-in-law's on the line, my daughter Marisol runs our Facebook because Lord knows I can't. We're full-service, real plates, and we're open all year, not just when the summer people come. I am proudly old-school. I barely touch my phone, so understand me when I say I'm tired of "solutions." Every app someone talked me into cost money, needed constant fiddling, and then showed the wrong information anyway.

Here's what I actually need, plain: my hours have to be RIGHT everywhere a visitor might look — the app, the town kiosk, Google — and I need to fix them in ten seconds from my phone, or let Marisol or a waiter do it, without calling anybody. Last spring we closed early for my cousin's funeral and the internet still said "open," so a whole ferry-load of walk-ons trudged up the hill to a locked door and left mad reviews. That cannot happen again. And when 200 people walk off a boat all at once, I want a heads-up ten minutes before — not a dining room that goes from empty to chaos with three people on the floor. If this app does those things, stays free to me, and gets out of my way, I'm in. If it's one more thing to maintain, I won't be.

### Danny Cho

> _"If I can post an event ONCE and it hits the town calendar, Instagram, Facebook, and Google without me copy-pasting it four times, I'm in — otherwise it's just one more dashboard I'll stop logging into by August."_

I'm Danny, co-owner of Friends & Neighbors Brewing right off the main strip in Kingston. We're open year-round, but our patio and our whole vibe swing hard with the seasons — dead-quiet Tuesdays in February, then a July 4th where the ferry dumps a few hundred walk-ons on us in twelve-minute waves. We've got no kitchen, so our entire food strategy is rotating food trucks and sending people down the block to Grub Hut or Los Tres when the truck's not here. I run all my own marketing: I shoot the reels, I make the event graphics, I answer the DMs. I'm the tech-comfortable one in this coalition — I'll happily wire up an API, I use Untappd and Square, and I'll pay a few bucks a month if the ROI is obvious.

Here's my problem: I am drowning in duplicate posting. Every trivia night, tap takeover, and live-music Friday gets typed FOUR separate times — Instagram, Facebook, Google Business Profile, and the Chamber's town calendar — and half the time the town one is stale because I forgot. I have zero real data on whether any of it works: did the 300 people who walked past us off the 5:15 boat actually come in, or did they all flow to the coffee shop? What I need from this app is brutally simple to say and apparently hard to get: post an event once and have it syndicate everywhere, a live tap list and 'who's the food truck today' people can check before they walk over, analytics I actually own, and a real channel to cross-promote with the other Kingston businesses instead of us all shouting into our own little silos. If it becomes just one more thing I have to babysit, I'm out.

### Margaret "Meg" Sorensen

> _"If it books my tastings, gates the 21+, and doesn't skim a commission off every pour, I'm in — otherwise it's just another dashboard I'll forget to log into."_

I'm Meg Sorensen, and I own Cove & Anchor Tasting Room a couple blocks off the Kingston strip. It's me, my husband on weekends, and one or two part-time pourers in season. We're built for the summer-and-fall crush — ferry walk-ons wander up when a boat lands, and on a good July Saturday we go from empty to a wall-to-wall room in about twelve minutes, then dead again. That whiplash is the whole problem. Right now I take tasting reservations over email and a paper book by the register, my wine club lives on a clipboard, and private-event inquiries land in an inbox I don't check until Monday. I've turned away walk-ins while a table I reserved by email never showed. I'm moderately comfortable with tech — I can run Square and post to Instagram — but I don't have time to babysit a complicated system, and I've been burned by booking tools that wanted a per-cover commission on top of a monthly fee. On a $12 flight of wine, a booking cut is real money I don't have.

What I need from this app is simple and specific: let visitors reserve a tasting (with a small deposit or card hold so they actually show), age-gate anything involving alcohol, let people join my wine club and mailing list without a clipboard, catch private-event inquiries in a real form, and — the fun part — cross-promote pairings with the restaurants down the strip so a flight at my place turns into dinner at Grub Hut and back. If it does that, syncs to the Google Calendar I already run my life on, and never takes a cut of my pours, I'll be its loudest fan at the next Chamber meeting.

### Carol Whitfield

> _"If a walk-on can't see me on their phone the second the boat lands, I don't exist for the next 12 minutes and I've already lost them."_

I'm Carol, and I own Sound & Cedar, a little gift and home boutique three minutes' walk up the hill from the Kingston dock. It's just me and one part-timer, so I'm the buyer, the bookkeeper, the window-dresser, and the person who sweeps the sidewalk. My whole living rides on foot traffic off the ferry: a boat lands, a wave of walk-ons comes up the ramp, and I have maybe twelve minutes before most of them wander back down to catch the next boat or decide 'there's nothing up here.' Half of them never come up the hill at all because they assume the whole town is the two blocks by the dock. That's the wall I hit every single day.

I'm not a tech person. I can post to Facebook and I've fumbled my way through Google, but I don't have time to babysit software and I sure don't have a marketing budget. So I need this app to be dead simple and basically free to me, and I need it to do one honest thing above all else: put me in front of somebody standing on the dock with their phone out, show them I'm OPEN and I'm a three-minute walk, and give them a reason to climb the hill and walk through my door. I am deeply wary of anything that turns my shop into a website order that ships from a warehouse. I don't want to compete with Amazon on Amazon's terms. I want the app to do the one thing Amazon can't: get a real person physically into my store while they're standing forty yards away.

### Priya Nair

> _"I pay the lodging tax that bankrolls this thing, so it had better turn ferry day-trippers into people who sleep here and give the Chamber numbers it can defend to LTAC without a fight."_

I'm Priya Nair and I run Cedarwood Inn, eight rooms two blocks up from the ferry dock. I'm the kind of owner who reads the channel-manager reports on a Sunday night: I know my occupancy, my ADR, my booking mix, and exactly how much Booking.com and Expedia skim off the top (15-18%, and it never stops hurting). I'm year-round, which makes me the odd one out in a town that empties in January, so I don't have the luxury of coasting on July. I need shoulder-season heads in beds. I'm comfortable with tech; give me an API, a CSV export, or an iCal feed and I'll wire it into my own tools myself. What I don't have is patience for anything that funnels my guests to an OTA or that produces marketing fluff the Chamber can't turn into a defensible grant report.

Here's my honest stake: I write the lodging-tax checks that partly fund this app, so I have skin in the game twice over. First, I want it to demonstrably CONVERT the ferry firehose of day-trippers into overnight guests ("the last boat back is 8:30, stay for the sunset and skip the scramble"). Second, I want it to make DIRECT booking the obvious path, not bury my rooms under OTA listings. And third, because I sit on the paying side of LTAC, I need the Chamber to be able to prove overnight impact and 50-mile / paid-lodging "tourist" reach in its grant reports, or the whole funding case collapses and takes the app with it. Get the ferry, tide, and last-boat data flawlessly right, because a guest stranded by our bad information is a one-star review with my inn's name on it, not the app's.

### Jordan Blake

> _"Give me one link I can drop in my Airbnb check-in message that answers every ferry-parking-wifi-dinner question for me — offline, because my cabins have one bar of signal — and I'll never text a guest at 11pm again."_

I host two cabins just up the hill from the Kingston ferry dock — I call them Cedar & Cove — and I do it entirely around a full-time day job. Guests are almost all overnight visitors: Seattle-side folks who walk on the Edmonds boat for a weekend, or drive-on families here for the Farmers Market, a Firehouse Theater show, or a quiet cove. I'm high-tech by disposition — I've wired up my own smart locks, I run pricing automation, I could stand up a guidebook site in a weekend — but 'could' is the trap. I have no time and I refuse to be on-call. Every hour spent answering 'which boat do I catch back?' for the fifth time is an hour I don't have.

What I need from this app is brutally simple: a self-service digital guest guide I can paste into my Airbnb and Vrbo check-in messages, that works when my guests have no signal, that I never have to hand-maintain. The Chamber already verifies ferry status, parking zones, and restaurant hours far better than I could on my own — I want to borrow that, not rebuild it. Two hard rules: it has to survive with a dead cell connection at my cabins, and it must NOT make me re-type everything I've already entered on Airbnb. If I have to keep two guidebooks in sync, I won't use it. If it just works and quietly makes me look professional to guests, I'll evangelize it to every host in Kingston.

### Hank Boyd

> _"I don't want an app, I want a boater to find my right phone number and my right hours before they call me at 9pm out of fuel. Get that dead right and big enough to read, and I'm in."_

I'm Hank Boyd. I've run Kingston Marine & Fuel down at the water for a long time — the fuel dock, pumpout, and a marine-service shop, me and two guys. My office is a VHF radio and a phone that rings. I trust channel 16 more than I'll ever trust a phone screen, and I'll tell you straight: I did not ask for an app. What I asked for, for years, is that a boater coming across from Edmonds or down from the San Juans can find the RIGHT hours and the RIGHT phone number before they're drifting outside the breakwater at dusk hollering for diesel. Half my headaches are people who found some stale hours on the internet, showed up after we closed, and now it's my fault.

So here's my deal with this thing. I am not going to log into a computer every week to fiddle with my listing — I don't have the time or the patience, and neither do my guys. If the Chamber wants me on board, the app has to carry the stuff a boater actually needs — fuel and pumpout hours, my phone, moorage and the transient-slip situation, tide, wind, and plain wayfinding from the dock up into town — and it has to be RIGHT, because a wrong tide or a wrong closing time on a boat is a real problem, not an inconvenience. Big text I can read without my glasses. Set-it-once so I'm not babysitting it. Get those things right and I'll stop being the skeptic. Get them wrong and I'll be the guy at every meeting saying I told you so.

### Sam Okafor

> _"If it can't get a latte in a commuter's hand before their boat leaves, and it doesn't take a 30% cut, I'm in — otherwise it's just another app I'll forget the password to."_

I'm Sam, and I open Dockside Coffee & Bakery at 5am, six days a week, a two-minute walk from the ferry terminal. My whole business is timing. The Edmonds boat lands, and ninety seconds later there's a line out my door — walk-on commuters who need a cortado and a scone but who will absolutely bail on the line if they think they'll miss the 6:40. I bake everything in-house starting at 4am, I run about six staff, and unlike most of the restaurants on the strip I'm year-round: my base is the daily commuter, and the summer visitor rush is gravy on top. My margins are thin. Coffee and pastry is a nickels-and-dimes business, and the delivery apps that wave 25-30% commissions at me would eat my entire profit on a $6 ticket.

I'm not afraid of technology — I run Square, I do my own social, I can figure things out — but I have zero spare minutes between 5 and 9am, so anything that needs babysitting during the rush is dead to me. What I actually need from this app is dead simple: let a commuter tap "order ahead" while they're still in their car or on the 104, tie the "ready by" time to the REAL ferry departure so it's hot when they grab it, and let them skip my line. Give me a punch-card so my regulars stick. And please, for the love of God, charge me a flat fee, not a slice of every cup. I'll pay for tools. I won't rent out my margin.

### Linda Cho-Ramirez

> _"Love the app, but it's a brochure for day-trippers who leave at 4pm; I'm trying to sell people who'd actually LIVE here, and right now the app gives them nothing."_

I'm Linda Cho-Ramirez, the broker-owner of North Sound Realty. I've been selling homes in north Kitsap for eleven years, and honestly, I don't sell houses so much as I sell Kingston. Nine out of ten of my buyers are relocators, usually a Seattle or Bellevue household that's tired of the price-per-square-foot math and has finally clued into the ferry. My whole job is answering the same twenty questions over and over: What are the schools like? How brutal is the commute, really? Is there a dentist, an urgent care, a vet? Can my kid do soccer here? Is there anything to DO in January? I'm the person who convinces someone that a 40-minute boat ride is a lifestyle, not a penalty. I do this with a binder of printouts, my own Google Doc of links, and a lot of driving-around.

I'm comfortable with technology: I run my listings on the NWMLS, I use DocuSign and a CRM, I build my own Facebook and Instagram ads, and I keep a Notion "Kingston relocation guide" I share with clients. So I'm not scared of this app, I'm frustrated by it. It's beautiful, and it's aimed entirely at the person who's here for four hours between ferries. What I need is a "Living Here" side of the house: accurate, current facts on schools, services, utilities, medical, worship, community groups, and the honest commute reality, plus a place to put my open houses so the town's own calendar actually reflects that homes are for sale. If the Chamber's mission is a thriving Kingston, then keeping the people who visit is at least as important as the people who leave.

### Tom Iverson

> _"If a 70-year-old volunteer can't update the vendor list on their phone from a folding chair in under five minutes, this app is useless to me — build it that simple or don't build it for us at all."_

I'm Tom Iverson. I manage the Kingston Public Market — folks call it the farmers market — down at Mike Wallace Marina Park right by the ferry dock, Sundays 10 to 3, May through October. I also sell produce there myself, so I'm hauling tables and tomatoes at 7 AM and answering "when do you open?" all day. We're a nonprofit run entirely by volunteers, a rotating crew of maybe a dozen people, most of them retired and wonderful and not one of them a "computer person." Our vendor lineup is roughly 30 stalls but it changes every single week — a farm's peppers aren't ready, a baker's out of town, a new soap lady shows up. Keeping that list current anywhere is a weekly headache, and right now it lives in my head, a paper clipboard, and a Facebook post I forget to update.

My honest relationship to technology is: suspicious and short on time. I've watched the Chamber roll out shiny things before that nobody could keep up, and the minute a tool needs a "login portal" and a manual, my volunteers quietly stop touching it and it goes stale — which is worse than nothing, because now the app is lying to visitors. So what I need from this app is dead simple and I will not budge on it: any volunteer, on their own phone, with no training, can update this Sunday's vendor lineup and blast a weather-cancellation alert in minutes. It has to be free to us — we run on tomato money and a bake sale. If it does those two things and shows people the SNAP/EBT match and where to park, we're in. If it's one more thing to maintain, we're out.

### Nadia Fischer

> _"If this app can pull our showtimes off five different platforms and stop bleeding us on ticket fees, I'll champion it to every arts board in the county — but only if it doesn't cost my volunteers a Saturday to learn."_

I'm Nadia Fischer, and I run Firehouse Theater and the Kingston Arts programs out of a building that's older than most of the town's opinions about it. We're a small nonprofit — a handful of part-time staff and a small army of volunteers who usher, run lights, hang the gallery shows, pour wine at openings, and sell tickets at a card table in the lobby. We're year-round, which makes us a little unusual in Kingston: when the ferry crowd thins out in November, we're the reason there's still a reason to come downtown on a Friday night. That's a fact I put in every grant application, and it's a fact I'd love this app to help me actually prove.

My relationship with technology is \"competent but exhausted.\" I can build an Eventbrite listing and a Facebook event and a Mailchimp blast, and I do, every single show — which is exactly the problem. Our showtimes live in five places and agree with each other maybe half the time. Ticketing platforms skim fees a nonprofit like ours genuinely cannot spare; every dollar Eventbrite takes is a dollar not spent on a kid's theater scholarship. I schedule dozens of volunteer shifts on a Google Sheet that somebody always overwrites. And when a grant report asks \"how many people did you serve,\" I'm reconstructing attendance from memory and torn ticket stubs. What I most need from this app is to be the ONE reliable place our events, tickets, volunteer signups, and membership live — with fees low enough that a nonprofit isn't subsidizing a tech company, and with numbers I can export straight into a grant report. If it does that, I'm your loudest advocate. If it's one more login my volunteers won't touch, it's dead to me.

### Marcus Reed

> _"A static map pin is a lie the moment I pull out of the lot — if this app can broadcast where the truck actually is TODAY and let people pre-order, I'm all in; if it can't stay real-time, I'd rather it not list me at all."_

I'm Marcus, and I run Rolling Cedar — a food truck that's in a different spot almost every day (Friends & Neighbors Brewing one night, the Kingston Farmers Market on Saturday, a private wedding up the hill the next), plus a small kayak-and-charter side gig on Appletree Cove that lives and dies by weather and tide. There's no storefront to find. My whole business is "where am I right now, and am I even open," and that answer changes daily and sometimes cancels an hour before I planned to fire the grill.

I'm very comfortable with tech — I already run Square for payments, post my location to Instagram Stories every morning, and juggle a booking spreadsheet for tours. So I'm not scared of a dashboard; I'm scared of a dashboard that's slow, that puts me on a fixed pin, or that shows visitors yesterday's info. The single thing I need from this app is a self-controlled, real-time TODAY status: my location, my hours, and my "open / sold out / cancelled" — set from my phone in ten seconds, showing on the map where I actually am, that goes stale automatically instead of lying. Pre-orders and weather/tide-aware tour booking would be gravy, and honestly I'm the person most excited about the future "order-ahead to the ferry line" idea. But if the location can't reflect reality the day of, none of the rest matters — a wrong pin is worse than no pin.

### Dr. Aisha Bello

> _"Great, another app for the ferry tourists — now show me one my patients and my neighbors would actually open on a rainy Tuesday in January."_

I'm Dr. Aisha Bello. I run Kingston Family Wellness on the resident side of town — a family clinic with a salon suite and a small fitness studio leasing space next to me. My patients are locals: young families, retirees, the people who actually live here through the quiet winter after the tourists go home. I'm year-round, I don't have a "peak season," and honestly most days I couldn't tell you whether the ferry is busy — but my staff and my patients absolutely can, because when the boat goes down or SR-104 backs up, half my afternoon appointments call to reschedule and my two employees who commute from Edmonds can't get here.

I'm reasonably comfortable with technology — I run an EHR, I book online, I can update a webpage — but I have zero spare hours to babysit yet another platform, and I'm careful about anything touching health information. Here's my problem with "Visit Kingston": it's built for the person here for three hours who wants a crepe and a webcam. That's fine. But there's no reason on earth for a Kingston resident to install it, and residents are 2,500 people who live here every single day. What I need is a genuine resident side — a trusted local directory that includes services like mine, a real emergency/alert channel I'd point my patients to when the ferry's out or the power's down, and simple, private ways for locals to make this their everyday town app. Build that, and I'll champion it. Bolt "residents" on as an afterthought tab, and I won't.
